Why plan ahead

A will is more than a document. It’s a plan you make for the people you love.

Wills

What happens if you don’t have one?

If you die without a will, the law decides who gets what. It follows a fixed formula, and the formula doesn’t know your family. A will replaces the formula with your own words.

When it’s worth writing or updating one

  • You got married, or moved in with a partner.
  • You had a child or a grandchild.
  • You bought a house, or started or sold a business.
  • You separated or divorced.
  • It has been a few years and you’re not sure your old one still fits.

You don’t need it all worked out first

We start with a short chat about your family and what you own. We talk through who should look after your children and where things should go. Then we write it in plain English and make sure it is signed and witnessed the way the law requires.

Ask us anything along the way. If we use a word you don’t recognise, that’s our mistake, not yours.

Enduring power of attorney

Who pays your bills if you can’t?

An enduring power of attorney lets you pick the person who looks after your money and paperwork if illness or injury means you can’t. You choose them yourself, while you still can.

What happens without one

  • No one can touch your accounts, not even family, without asking a tribunal first.
  • That takes months, and it costs money.
  • The tribunal picks who acts for you, and it may not be who you would have chosen.
  • The rent, the mortgage and the bills still have to be paid in the meantime.

Why now, and not later

You can only set one up while you can still make your own decisions. Once a crisis hits, it is usually too late.

Sorting it now, while everything is fine, keeps the choice in your hands rather than a tribunal’s. Set it up once, and it waits quietly until the day it is needed.

Pick the right person now, on your own terms.

Enduring guardianship

Who decides on your care if you can’t speak for yourself?

A power of attorney covers your money. An enduring guardian covers you — where you live, the treatment you get, the support you receive — if you’re ever unable to say so yourself.

Why it’s worth doing

Without one, your family may have to ask a tribunal to be appointed. It is slow, it is stressful, and the tribunal may not choose the person you would have.

Naming someone now means the people who know your wishes are the ones who get to act on them.

Valid when it counts

You need to be able to make your own decisions on the day you sign, so the document holds up later when it is needed.

Sorting it while you are well also lifts the weight of these decisions off your family at a hard time. Your voice stays in the room.

Put your care in hands that already know your wishes.
